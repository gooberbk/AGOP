Abstract
Context.Agriculture represents a vital pillar of the Algerian economy, yet the majority
of small and medium-scale farmers still rely on experience-based, manual decision-making
without access to modern data-driven tools. The global rise of smart farming and preci-
sion agriculture ÔÇö driven by mobile computing, artificial intelligence, and real-time data
collection ÔÇö creates a direct opportunity to deliver agricultural intelligence to farmers
through affordable smartphones, which have seen rapid adoption across Algeria in recent
years.
Problem.Farmers face daily decisions regarding irrigation, fertilization, and harvest-
ing without any structured support system. Existing smart farming platforms such as
Farmbrite, Agrivi, and Granular are subscription-based, designed for large-scale enter-
prises, and not adapted to Algerian crop types, languages, or contexts. The core gap
is the absence of a free, localised, and intelligent farming assistant accessible on a stan-
dard Android smartphone, capable of delivering AI-driven irrigation recommendations
grounded in real agronomic data.
Methodology.AGOP was developed as a cross-platform mobile application usingFlut-
ter(Dart) for the frontend andFastAPI(Python 3.11) for the RESTful backend, with
anSQLitedatabase managed throughSQLAlchemy ORM. The AI core integrates a
pre-trainedLightGBMmodel loaded viajoblibto predict crop yield under two scenar-
ios ÔÇö with and without irrigation ÔÇö and derives personalised irrigation recommendations
combined with liveOpenWeatherMapdata. The development followed theV-Model
methodology, pairing each design phase with a corresponding validation step.
Results.The resulting application provides farmers with a personalised home dashboard
displaying real-time weather metrics (temperature, humidity, wind, UV index, 5-day fore-
cast), a crop management module supporting multiple crop types and soil profiles with
growth-stage tracking, a task manager covering watering, fertilizing, and harvesting activ-
ities with completion tracking, and an AI-driven recommendation engine that computes
yield gain in tonnes per hectare and assigns a confidence level to each irrigation decision.
All 12 planned functional test cases passed successfully on Android devices, with API
response times consistently below 200 ms.
Keywords:smart farming, yield prediction, irrigation optimisation, water resource man-
agement, crop monitoring, LightGBM, gradient boosting, mobile agriculture, precision
agriculture, RESTful API.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1 Context
Agriculture is one of the most fundamental sectors of the Algerian economy, employing
a large portion of the rural population and contributing significantly to national food
security. Despite its importance, the sector lags behind in the adoption of modern tech-
nologies, particularly when compared to industrialised nations where precision agriculture
and smart farming have already transformed how crops are managed.
Globally, the emergence of intelligent farming solutions ÔÇö combining mobile applications,
the Internet of Things (IoT), and artificial intelligence (AI) ÔÇö has demonstrated mea-
surable improvements in crop yields, water usage efficiency, and overall farm profitability.
Smartphone penetration in Algeria has grown substantially in recent years, making mobile
applications one of the most viable and cost-effective channels for delivering agricultural
intelligence directly to farmers in the field.
In parallel, advances in machine learning, particularly gradient boosting algorithms such
asLightGBM(Light Gradient Boosting Machine), have made it possible to train highly
accurate predictive models on agricultural datasets and deploy them within lightweight
backend services. These models can predict crop yield under different conditions and gen-
erate data-driven irrigation recommendations that would otherwise require the expertise
of an agronomist.
1.2 Problem Statement
Despite the availability of global smart farming platforms, Algerian farmers face a specific
and largely unaddressed challenge: the complete absence of a free, locally adapted, and
intelligent farming assistant accessible on a standard Android smartphone.
The key problems are as follows:
ÔÇóManual, intuition-based decisions:Most farmers decide when and how much
to irrigate based on habit and experience, without considering current weather fore-
casts, soil conditions, or crop growth stages. This leads to over-watering or under-
watering, both of which reduce yield and waste resources.
ÔÇóNo structured task management:There is no system to schedule, track, or re-
mind farmers of critical farming activities such as fertilization or harvesting, causing
missed windows that directly impact productivity.
ÔÇóInaccessibility of existing solutions:Platforms such as Farmbrite, Agrivi, and
Granular require paid subscriptions, are designed for large-scale agribusinesses, and

ÔÇóAbsence of AI-powered irrigation advice:No existing free tool combines real-

The primary client of AGOP is theAlgerian farmerÔÇö an individual responsible for

ÔÇóManages multiple crop types (wheat, tomato, corn, etc.) across different fields with

ÔÇóCannot afford expensive subscription-based agricultural platforms.
A secondary client is theAI recommendation engineacting as an internal system

for their daily farming decisions. The consequences are direct and measurable:
ÔÇóInefficient irrigation:Without weather-aware guidance, farmers over-water or
under-water their crops, wasting water and reducing yield.
ÔÇóNo activity tracking:Watering and fertilization dates are not recorded, making
it impossible to evaluate past decisions or plan future ones.

This chapter translates the requirements identified in Chapter 2 into a concrete system

3.2 System Architecture
3.2.1 Overview
AGOP follows a classicalthree-tier client-server architecture, separating concerns
across a presentation layer (mobile frontend), a logic layer (REST API backend), and a
data layer (relational database). This separation ensures that each tier can evolve inde-
pendently: the Flutter frontend can be updated without touching the FastAPI backend,
and the database schema can be migrated without rewriting any mobile code.

4.2.2 Technology Choices Justification
Flutter (Frontend).Flutter was selected over native Android (Java/Kotlin) or React
Native because it compiles to native ARM code, delivers consistent 60 fps rendering, and
supports a single codebase for both Android and future iOS deployment. Its rich widget
library enabled building polished, responsive screens ÔÇö dashboards, progress bars, and
crop cards ÔÇö without third-party UI kits.
FastAPI (Backend).FastAPI was chosen over Flask or Django REST Framework
for three reasons: automatic OpenAPI documentation generation, native asynchronous
support via PythonÔÇÖsasync/awaitmodel, and Pydantic-powered schema validation that
eliminates boilerplate input-checking code. Its performance under concurrent requests
satisfies NFR-02 (<200ms response time).
LightGBM (AI Model).LightGBM was preferred over heavier alternatives (Random
Forest, XGBoost, neural networks) because it produces state-of-the-art regression accu-
racy on tabular agricultural data while loading in under 50 ms at startup and predicting
in under 5 ms per call. This makes it ideal for embedding inside a REST endpoint without
a dedicated ML server.
SQLite + SQLAlchemy ORM.SQLite requires zero server configuration, making
deployment to a single server or developer machine trivial. SQLAlchemy ORM abstracts
all SQL queries into Python objects, enforces referential integrity through foreign keys,
and allows migration to PostgreSQL in production without changing any application code
(NFR-06, NFR-09).

Chapter 5
Conclusion and Future Work
5.1 Conclusion
This report has presented the full design and implementation ofAGOP, a free, mobile-
first, AI-powered farming application targeting small and medium-scale Algerian farmers.
From the initial problem statement to the final functional test campaign, each phase of
the project was conducted according to theV-Modelmethodology, ensuring that every
design decision was paired with a concrete validation step.
5.1.1 Summary of Achievements
The project successfully delivered all six objectives stated in Chapter 1. The key achieve-
ments are summarised below.
Cross-platform mobile application.A fully functional Android application was built
usingFlutter(Dart), providing four integrated screens ÔÇö Home Dashboard, Crops,
Tasks, and AI Recommendations ÔÇö navigable by a non-technical farmer without prior
training. The interface displays real-time GPS-based weather data including temperature,
humidity, wind speed, UV index, and a 5-day forecast powered by theOpenWeath-
erMapAPI.
AI-driven irrigation recommendation engine.A pre-trainedLightGBMmodel
was successfully integrated into the FastAPI backend through a dual-scenario inference
pipeline. For each recommendation request, the engine predicts crop yield under irrigated
and non-irrigated conditions, computes the yield gain in tonnes per hectare, applies a
meteorological override rule for significant rainfall forecasts (>15mm in 48 h), and
returns a confidence-rated action (high / moderate / low) to the farmer. This constitutes
the first AI-powered, yield-based irrigation advisor freely accessible on a standard Android
smartphone in the Algerian agricultural context.
Crop and task management.The application allows farmers to register multiple
crops with detailed profiles (crop type, soil type, field area, planting date) and auto-
matically tracks their growth stage progression. A task management module enables the
scheduling and completion tracking of watering, fertilizing, and harvesting activities, with
due dates and completion timestamps recorded per crop.
ScalableRESTfulbackend.TheFastAPIbackendexposesafullydocumentedREST
API organised into five independent module layers (routes, CRUD, models, schemas, ser-
vices), backed by anSQLitedatabase managed throughSQLAlchemy ORMwith full

referential integrity. All endpoints responded within the 200 ms non-functional threshold,
with most CRUD operations completing in under 25 ms.
Complete validation.All 12 planned functional test cases passed successfully on An-
droid devices, covering user registration and authentication, crop lifecycle management,
task creation and completion, and both irrigation recommendation scenarios. All 9 non-
functional requirements ÔÇö covering performance, security, usability, reliability, scalability,

5.1.2 Lessons Learned
Several important lessons emerged from the development process. Adopting the V-Model
from the outset enforced a discipline of writing test cases before implementation, which
caught several edge cases in the registration and recommendation logic early. Centralising
all HTTP communication in a single FlutterApiServiceclass significantly simplified de-
bugging during integration. Finally, loading the LightGBM model once at startup rather
than per request was a critical optimisation: it reduced the recommendation endpoint
latency from several seconds to under 10 ms of pure inference time.
5.2 Future Work
While AGOP fulfils all of its initial objectives, several directions for improvement and
extension have been identified during development and testing.
5.2.1 Plant Disease Detection via Image Recognition
The most impactful near-term extension would be the integration of acomputer vision
module allowing farmers to photograph a diseased crop leaf and receive an automatic
diagnosis. A convolutional neural network (CNN) ÔÇö such as a fine-tunedMobileNetV3
orEfficientNetÔÇö trained on the open-sourcePlantVillagedataset could identify the
disease type and severity, and recommend treatment. This would transform AGOP from
an irrigation advisor into a comprehensive crop health platform.
5.2.2 IoT Sensor Integration
Replacing GPS-based weather API calls within-field IoT sensors(soil moisture probes,
temperature loggers, rain gauges) would make recommendations significantly more pre-
cise. A lightweightMQTTbroker could ingest sensor readings in real time and feed them
directly into the LightGBM feature pipeline, eliminating the dependency on external API
rate limits and improving accuracy for micro-climates that regional weather stations do
not capture.
5.2.3 Offline Mode
Farmers in rural Algeria frequently operate with limited or intermittent mobile connec-
tivity. A future version of AGOP should implement anoffline-first architectureusing
FlutterÔÇÖs local storage (sqflitepackage) to cache the last known weather data, crop
39
AGOP ÔÇô AI-Powered FarmingCHAPTER 5. CONCLUSION AND FUTURE WORK
profiles, and recommendations locally. Pending tasks and farming logs would be queued
and synchronised with the backend automatically once connectivity is restored.
5.2.4 Multilingual Support (Arabic and French)
The current application is built entirely in English. Given that the target users are Al-
gerian farmers whose primary languages areArabic(Darija or Modern Standard) and
French, adding full internationalisation using FlutterÔÇÖsflutter_localizationspack-
age is a high-priority improvement. The backend API responses and recommendation
messages would also need to be localised accordingly.
5.2.5 Continuous Model Retraining
The LightGBM model is currently static: it was trained once on a fixed agricultural
dataset and does not improve with usage. A future pipeline could collect anonymised
farming logs and actual harvest yields from consenting users, and retrain the model pe-
riodically usingfederated learningor a centralised retraining job, so that predictions
become progressively more accurate for Algerian crop varieties, soil types, and climate
conditions.
5.2.6 Weather-Aware Notification System
A push notification service ÔÇö implemented viaFirebase Cloud Messaging (FCM)
ÔÇö could proactively alert farmers when critical conditions are detected: an approaching
frost, a sudden temperature spike, or a 48-hour rain window that makes immediate har-
vesting advisable. This would extend AGOP from a reactive tool into a proactive farming
assistant.
5.2.7 Production Deployment and Scalability
The current deployment runs on a local Uvicorn server. A production release would
require containerisation withDocker, orchestration withKubernetesor a managed
cloud platform (AWS, Azure, or a local Algerian cloud provider), migration from SQLite
toPostgreSQLfor concurrent multi-user access, and the implementation of JWT-based
authentication to replace the current session model.
In conclusion, AGOP demonstrates that a meaningful and technically sound precision
agriculture tool can be built within a single academic semester using open-source tech-
nologies, without any infrastructure cost to the end user. The foundation laid by this
project ÔÇö a modular backend, a validated ML inference pipeline, and a farmer-friendly
mobile interface ÔÇö provides a solid basis for all of the extensions described above, and
positions AGOP as a viable starting point for a real-world smart farming product serving
the Algerian agricultural sector.
